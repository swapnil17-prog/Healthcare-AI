# Explanations of Common Lab Values

Understanding your laboratory results is a key component of tracking your health and risk predictions. Here is a guide explaining what HbA1c, Cholesterol, and Creatinine represent.

### HbA1c (Hemoglobin A1c)
The HbA1c test measures the average amount of glucose (blood sugar) attached to your red blood cells over the past 2 to 3 months. It is used as a primary diagnostic indicator and monitoring tool for prediabetes and diabetes.
- **Normal**: Less than 5.7%
- **Prediabetes**: 5.7% to 6.4%
- **Diabetes**: 6.5% or higher on two separate tests.
Keeping HbA1c in check is important to prevent long-term diabetic complications.

### Cholesterol Profile
Cholesterol is a waxy substance found in your blood that your body needs to build healthy cells, but high levels can increase your risk of heart disease. A standard lipid panel measure:
- **Total Cholesterol**: The overall amount of cholesterol in your blood. Normal is generally less than 200 mg/dL.
- **LDL (Low-Density Lipoprotein)**: Often called the "bad" cholesterol. High levels can build up in artery walls. Optimal is less than 100 mg/dL.
- **HDL (High-Density Lipoprotein)**: Often called the "good" cholesterol. It helps clear bad cholesterol from the blood. Higher numbers (above 50 mg/dL) are protective.
- **Triglycerides**: A type of fat in the blood. Normal is less than 150 mg/dL.

### Creatinine and Kidney Function
Creatinine is a chemical waste product produced by muscle metabolism and filtered out of the body by healthy kidneys. A creatinine blood test measures how well your kidneys are filtering waste.
- **Normal Range**: Generally 0.6 to 1.2 mg/dL for men and 0.5 to 1.1 mg/dL for women, though this varies by lab.
- **Elevated Creatinine**: Can indicate that the kidneys are not filtering blood as efficiently as they should, requiring further clinical diagnostic testing.
